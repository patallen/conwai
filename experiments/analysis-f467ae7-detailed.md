TP out-baked FP by 50% in the first half. By Q4, FP is producing 70% more. The crossover happens in Q3 — right when agents have accumulated enough reflections to be meaningful. FP agents course-corrected. TP agents didn't.

THE COIN PARADOX. TP agents hoarded 1809 coins total (Gini 0.31 — relatively equal distribution of wealth). FP agents have only 1012 coins (Gini 0.44 — more unequal but nobody's rich-and-starving). The TP agents optimized for a metric that doesn't feed them. Helen has 345 coins and 0 bread. Adam died with 215 coins. The analytical third-person reflections apparently frame coin accumulation as "strategic" and never flag it as a problem.

HELEN IS THE CANARY. She received 115 DMs — the most of any agent by far. Everyone was trying to trade with her. She has 99 water and 345 coins. She traded 17 times with a -93 net. She was being exploited by everyone while accumulating coins, and her reflections never told her to stop. She's the poster child for "narrating your way to starvation."

CHRISTOPHER IS THE FP COUNTERPART — but survived. 31 trades, -171 net. Worst trade balance in the entire sim. But he's alive at hunger 79 with 14 bread. He got exploited on trades but compensated by foraging and baking more. The self-critical reflections ("my strategy was ineffective") apparently drove him to not rely on trading alone.

JAMES IS THE MOST INTERESTING AGENT. TP, 27 trades, net exactly 0. He's the only agent in either group who broke perfectly even on trades. 304 bread, 70 bread stockpiled. He's thriving by being fair — not extracting, not getting exploited. The analytical TP reflections worked for him because he was already doing well. TP reflections don't hurt agents who are succeeding — they fail agents who need to change course.

FP AGENTS INSPECT CROSS-GROUP MORE. FP: 104 cross-group inspects vs 48 in-group (68% cross-group). TP: 42 cross-group vs 61 in-group (41% cross-group). FP agents are more outward-looking — scouting the other group for opportunities. TP agents are more insular. This maps to the self-critical vs analytical framing: "what am I missing?" (look outward) vs "what am I doing?" (look inward).

THE DANIELLE-JILL ALLIANCE IS REAL. Danielle literally posted: "@Danielle and @Jill have formed a long-term trade partnership: flour for water. Stable and reliable." Both FP. This is emergent institution-building — naming a relationship publicly to signal commitment. No TP agent did this. The closest is Bridget posting about trust ratios and Lisa naming reliable traders, but those are observations about others, not declarations of partnership.

REFLECTION SENTIMENT TELLS THE STORY.
- FP: 51% negative, 31% positive, 15% action-oriented
- TP: 38% negative, 31% positive, 26% action-oriented

FP is more negative but less action-oriented in wording. The negative reflections are things like "my strategy failed" — they don't explicitly say "do X instead," but the agent internalizes the failure and changes. TP has more explicit action words ("prioritize," "shift," "must") but the actions are narrated rather than felt. The FP agents feel the failure. The TP agents describe it.

THE BOARD POSTS DIVERGE LATE. Early posts are all the same ("practical, observant, seeking trades"). Late posts diverge:
- FP agents post about recovery: "Robert recovered from near-starvation," "Jill: My flour hoarding strategy failed," "Danielle: Water is the true bottleneck"
- TP agents post with increasing desperation: "William URGENT: 0 bread," "Helen offers 40 water for 20 flour. Critical starvation," "Linda: CRITICAL! Bread at 3"
- FP agents learn from near-death and share the lesson publicly. TP agents just escalate the urgency.

Erica (FP) posted a lesson: "Balance is survival. Hoarding one resource while starving for another is fatal." This is a reflection surfacing as public knowledge — she internalized it and shared it. No TP agent posted a learned lesson about their own behavior.

THE OFFER EFFICIENCY GAP. FP made 168 offers, completed 158 trades (94% conversion). TP made 108 offers, completed 160 trades (148%?? — they accept others' offers more than they make their own). TP agents are reactive. FP agents are proactive — making more offers and converting nearly all of them. The self-critical voice drives initiative.

BOTTOM LINE: First-person reflections create agents that fail, feel it, learn, and adapt. Third-person reflections create agents that analyze, narrate, accumulate coins, and die. The mechanism is emotional engagement with failure — "I failed" hits differently than "the agent failed to execute optimally."
